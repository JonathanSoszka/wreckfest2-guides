# Corner Types Reference — Source of Truth

Real-world racing/rally and sim-racing consensus on the **common corner shapes and how to drive each**,
and how the approach shifts across surfaces. This is the reference the *Corner Types* lesson is checked
against. It builds on `car-control-reference.md` (the grip budget, weight transfer, the late-apex
"square-off", slide control) — read that first; this applies those fundamentals per corner shape.

**Scope & caveat.** Defined for grip driving (circuit) and rally, carried into sim racing. Wreckfest 2
is arcade physics with heavy contact, so treat these as the *default approach*, then validate in-game.
Surfaces run loose (mud/gravel) to grippy (asphalt); most Wreckfest tracks are loose or mixed. Gamepad
note: coarse inputs favour early, small, smooth corrections.

## The rule that governs every corner

**Prioritise the exit onto the longest straight — slow in, fast out.** The corner (or the last element
of a linked sequence) that feeds a straight sets your speed for the whole next section, so a tidy exit
beats a fast apex. Almost every technique below is this rule applied to a particular shape and surface:
carry less speed into the part that matters least, get the car pointed, and be at full throttle earliest
where it counts.

## The corner shapes

Each: what it is, how to drive it on grip, and the loose-surface adjustment.

- **Hairpin** — a very tight, slow ~180° turn. Brake early and hard in a straight line, slow entry,
  **late apex**, rotate the car, then power out. Slow-in/fast-out is most extreme here.
  *Loose:* lean on rotation — a lift or trailed brake to swing the tail, the **Scandinavian flick /
  pendulum** (and the handbrake on the tightest) to get it turned, then drive out.
- **Sweeper** — a long, fast, roughly constant-radius curve. Little or no braking; one smooth arc,
  steady throttle, small inputs, carry momentum. *Loose:* hold a committed, constant-angle slide rather
  than sawing at corrections.
- **Chicane / esses** — a quick linked left–right (or right–left). Brake straight beforehand, then
  **sacrifice the first apex** to straighten the run and nail the exit of the *last* element that feeds
  the straight; manage the side-to-side weight transfer and don't over-rotate into the second part.
  *Loose:* even more about not upsetting the car between the direction changes.
- **Double-apex** — one long corner you touch the inside of twice. Treat it as a single continuous
  corner: patient through the middle, and set the second apex up for the exit.
- **Increasing-radius (opens up)** — sharpest early, then it widens. You can apex earlier and get on
  the power sooner because the track opens toward the exit.
- **Decreasing-radius (tightens)** — sharpest at the *end*: the classic trap. Apex early and you run
  wide off the exit. **Late apex**, patience, brake/trail deeper, and wait for it to open before you
  commit the throttle. *Loose:* same, exaggerated — the run-wide is a slide off the outside.
- **Off-camber** — the road slopes *away* from the turn, so there's less grip and the car pushes wide.
  Slow more, trail-brake a little harder, sometimes turn in slightly earlier, and be gentle. *Loose:*
  treacherous — leave a big margin, expect it to wash or slide out.
- **Crest / blind** — the car goes light over a rise (front unloads → understeer, then rear unloads →
  oversteer). Don't ask for lateral grip while it's light: get the braking and most of the steering
  done before or after the crest, run a **later apex**, and use reference points when you can't see the
  exit. *Loose:* even lighter and bouncier — straighten the car over the crest.
- **Banked** — camber adds grip. Turn in a touch earlier to get down into the banking, stay in it as
  long as you can, and carry more speed. (In Wreckfest this is mostly the speedway/oval banking.)

## Terrain layer

- **Asphalt (grip):** high, consistent grip — precise lines, trail-braking, exit priority.
- **Gravel / dirt (loose):** lower, *dynamic* grip — lean harder on the late-apex square-off, rotate
  the car on entry (lift, brake, or flick), steer with the throttle, and leave bigger margins on
  off-camber and crests. The **Scandinavian flick / pendulum** is the loose-surface tool for tight
  corners and hairpins.
- **Mud (extreme loose):** everything softer, slower, and earlier; abrupt inputs bog or spin you.

## How this maps to Wreckfest 2

- Arcade physics and contact, but the shapes and the exit-priority rule hold. Loose surfaces dominate,
  so rotation-on-entry and a clean exit matter more than a perfect apex.
- Wreckfest venues cover most of these: FinnCross / European Rallycross (mixed, chicane-like links),
  Savolax (loose sweepers and tight quarry corners), Speedway / UK Banger (banked ovals and figure-eight
  crossovers).
- Gamepad: the flick and trail-braking all work, but keep every correction small and early.
