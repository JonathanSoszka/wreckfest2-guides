#### Why these numbers

The **Differential** is the biggest lever on a rear-drive car, and it runs opposite to instinct:
**locked on loose, opener on grip.** On mud and gravel, low grip means an open diff just spins the
inside rear away — locking it drives both wheels and puts the torque down (the loose surface still
lets you rotate on the throttle). On asphalt, a locked diff snaps both rear wheels loose together,
so opening it up keeps the exit progressive.

Everything else follows the surface: soft/high/less-brake on loose, stiff/low/more-brake on grip.
The rear stays a touch softer than the front (springs and bar) so the torque hooks up on exit
rather than snapping the tail loose.

Brake Pressure's scale runs 50–150%, so 100% is mid-travel, not the maximum.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the lowest-grip surface — soft, draggy, full of ruts. The rear sinks and spins and power
oversteer comes early, so soften the springs and bars, raise the ride height to clear the ruts, and
**lock the diff up**: on low grip a tight diff drives both rear wheels instead of spinning the
inside one away, and the loose surface still lets you steer with the throttle.

A bogged-down car is hard to recover — feed the throttle smoothly rather than stabbing it.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel is the middle ground and sits closest to the hybrid setup — the changes are small. The car
already slides predictably; steer it with the throttle and keep corrections small, which matters on
a pad where a big stick input overshoots.

Let the car rotate on power and drive out of it; do not try to hold it dead straight.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt has real grip, so the springs and bars stiffen, brake pressure climbs, and the car drops
closer to the road — and the **diff opens up**: on high grip a locked diff breaks both rear wheels
loose together, so a looser diff keeps the exit progressive.

Keep some compliance even here — Wreckfest's tarmac is bumpy and kerbed, and a mid-corner bump can
step the rear out unexpectedly.

</Fragment>

<h1>Generic RWD Setup</h1>

<p class="tags"><span class="tag tag-rwd">RWD</span></p>

<p class="lead">A starting setup and the reasoning behind it for any rear-wheel-drive car, in the game's own units.</p>

<div class="callout"><p>Have a car-specific guide? Those refine these numbers for a single car. Otherwise start here.</p></div>

The baseline for rear-drive cars — the muscle coupes and the heavier vehicles. The job is a car
that **rotates into tight corners without spinning** when you get on the power over a bump. The
**Hybrid** tab on the left is an all-round setup that does OK on any surface, in the game's real
sliders and units; each surface tab shows what to change from it. Everything here is a starting
point — change one thing, drive the same corner twice, feel it. Gamepad players: Steering Ratio one
notch slower than shown.

## Reading the presets

The filled marker is the setting, the tick is the hybrid value, and the coloured number is the
change from it (green higher, blue lower). Each tab's own **Why these changes** panel — below its
settings — explains the reasoning for that surface.
