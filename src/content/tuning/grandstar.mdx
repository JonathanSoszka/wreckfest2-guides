<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Grandstar is a heavy, very nose-heavy minivan with a high centre of gravity — strong understeer
and a real rollover risk. You cannot make it agile, so make it stable and use the mass. **Stiff
everywhere and low as allowed:** firm springs and bars to control the roll, and the ride height
dropped further than a lighter FWD to keep the tall body from tipping.

The rear runs very stiff — it both rotates the heavy nose and cuts the roll. The **differential**
governs the driven front wheels: locked on loose to drag the mass out of corners, opener on grip so
it stops fighting your steering. Brakes sit well forward since the huge nose weight does the
stopping, and you brake early because there is a lot of car to slow down.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud plus a heavy understeering nose is the worst case — the driven front digs and pushes wide.
Soften slightly and raise the ride height off the lowest setting to clear the ruts, and **lock the
diff up** so both front wheels haul the mass out.

Brake early and get the nose pointed on the entry lift; once this thing is understeering under power
in mud, nothing brings it back but patience.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel gives you a little rotation to work with and sits close to the hybrid setup. Lift off to
bring the heavy nose round, then feed the power in smoothly and let the mass carry you out.

Keep the rear bar very stiff — it is the only thing rotating this nose — and remember the mass is an
asset in traffic; a well-placed Grandstar shrugs off contact that spins lighter cars.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt is where the high CG and nose weight fight you hardest — roll and exit understeer both peak.
Stiffen everything further, drop the car as low as allowed, and **open the diff** so it stops adding
understeer as it fights your steering.

Add front negative camber to keep the outside front planted through the roll, and stay smooth on
turn-in; a sudden input tips weight onto that tall outside corner and the nose just pushes.

</Fragment>
