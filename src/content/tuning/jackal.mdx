<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Jackal is the lightest and twitchiest muscle car — a short wheelbase over a live rear axle, so
it snaps into oversteer when you lift and hops the axle over bumps. Everything here adds rear grip
and calms the tail: the softest rear springs and rear **anti-roll bar** of the group, a slightly
raised rear to let the axle work, and toe-in to steady it on lift.

The **Differential** matters as much as ever, but the lock is eased back — a fully locked diff
snaps this light car loose on power. It still runs locked on loose and opener on grip; the coast
side is turned up to steady the lift-off snap. Reward smooth inputs: this car punishes a stab or a
sudden lift. Brake Pressure's scale runs 50–150%, so 100% is mid-travel, not the maximum.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the lowest-grip surface, and a light car both spins and locks easily — ease the brake
pressure and feed the throttle gently. Soften everything and raise the ride height to clear the
ruts, and lock the diff up more than you would on grip to drive both rear wheels.

Smooth is fast here — a stab of power or a sharp lift lights the tail up before the loose surface
can catch it.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel suits the light Jackal well and sits close to the hybrid. The loose surface takes the edge
off the snap, so you can lean on the rotation — steer it with the throttle and keep corrections
small, which matters doubly on a twitchy car and a pad where a big stick input overshoots.

Keep your inputs smooth and let it rotate on power; do not try to hold it dead straight.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt gives the Jackal real grip, but the live axle still hops over kerbs and the tail still
snaps on a greedy lift. The springs and bars stiffen and the car drops, but the rear stays the
softest end and rides a touch higher than usual to let the axle absorb bumps instead of hopping.
The **diff opens up** further on grip so it can't break both rear wheels loose together.

Be smooth with the throttle and gentle over the kerbs — a mid-corner bump can step this short car
out before you feel it coming.

</Fragment>
