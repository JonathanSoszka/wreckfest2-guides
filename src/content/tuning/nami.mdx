<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Nami is tiny and light with a short wheelbase, so it rotates beautifully — and bites back just
as fast. The trap is over-tuning for rotation: it already turns, and it makes so little power that a
too-loose rear just scrubs away its meagre exit drive. So you manage the tail from both ends. The
**Differential** carries more coast lock than usual to catch the quick lift-off bite, with only
moderate power lock so you don't waste the drive you have.

Everything else is a steadying hand on a nervous car — soft-ish rear bar, rear toe-in, honest brake
balance. Follow the surface for the rest: locked diff and soft/high on loose, opener diff and
stiff/low on grip. The **Final Drive** stays short to keep the peaky little four in its powerband.
Brake Pressure's scale runs 50–150%, so 100% is mid-travel, not the maximum.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the lowest-grip surface, and on this little car the rear steps out with almost no warning.
Soften the springs and bars, raise the ride height to clear the ruts, and **lock the diff up**: on
low grip a tight diff drives both rear wheels instead of spinning the inside one away, and the loose
surface still lets you steer with the throttle.

Carry your speed in — the Nami has next to no power to make it back up. Momentum through the corner,
not drive out of it, is how you win here.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel is the middle ground and sits closest to the hybrid setup — the changes are small. The Nami
is at its most fun here, rotating on the throttle, but the bite is quick; keep corrections small,
which matters double on a pad where a big stick input overshoots.

Ride the momentum and stay smooth. A tidy, flowing line beats a lurid slide every time in a car
this underpowered.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

On asphalt the Nami finally has grip to lean on, and it can carry real corner speed for such a small
car. The springs and bars stiffen, brake pressure climbs, and the car drops closer to the road — and
the **diff opens up**: on high grip a locked diff breaks both rear wheels loose together, so a
looser diff keeps the exit progressive.

Win on line and momentum, not slides. Brake in a straight line — that quick rear bite is unforgiving
if you trail into the corner and unsettle it.

</Fragment>
