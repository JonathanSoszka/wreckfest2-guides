#### Why these numbers

All-wheel drive changes the problem. Traction out of corners is rarely the issue — power goes to all
four tyres — so an AWD car puts power down on loose surfaces better than a FWD or RWD car. What you
tune instead is **balance**: an AWD car can understeer like a FWD car or rotate like a RWD car, and
the setup decides which.

The **anti-roll bars are your main balance trim** — stiffen the rear to add rotation, the front to
calm the car. Keep both soft-ish; compliance keeps all four tyres on the ground, where the traction
advantage actually lives. The springs sit close front-to-rear because the car is balanced by design.

The **Differential** still locks on loose for traction and opens on grip, but it matters less than
on a two-wheel-drive car — trim balance with the bars first, then the diff.

Camber is spread evenly front-to-rear, since all four tyres both drive and corner.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is where AWD shines brightest — all four wheels claw for grip where a FWD or RWD car bogs down,
so you carry drive out of corners that would strand the others. Soften and raise everything to keep
the tyres planted, and lock the diff up for traction.

The catch is weight: a heavy AWD car can still plough straight on. Lean on the traction and feed the
power in confidently off the corner where the others cannot.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel suits AWD well and sits close to the hybrid setup. The car is planted and carries more corner
speed than a FWD or RWD car, because all four tyres share the cornering and driving load.

Trim rotation with the rear anti-roll bar rather than reaching for the diff every time.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt gives you grip to push hard, and the car feels fast and stable. The failure mode is
understeer, so stiffen up, lower the car, and **open the diff** so it does not fight your steering —
then add rear anti-roll bar if you still need the nose to bite.

Keep the camber budget fairly even front-to-rear, since all four tyres are still working.

</Fragment>

All-wheel drive rarely struggles for traction — the question is balance, and the setup answers it.
The **Hybrid** tab on the left is an all-round setup that does OK on any surface, in the game's real
sliders and units; each surface tab shows what to change from it. Gamepad players: Steering Ratio
one notch slower than shown.

## Reading the presets

The filled marker is the setting, the tick is the hybrid value, and the coloured number is the
change from it (green higher, blue lower). Each tab's own **Why these changes** panel — below its
settings — explains the reasoning for that surface.
