<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Popper is tiny, light, and rear-engined, so the engine sits behind the rear axle and every
setup choice is about steadying the tail. It has almost no straight-line speed, but on a tight
technical track that barely matters — it points and darts. The **Final Drive** runs very short to
keep the tiny twin in its powerband; gear it any taller and it falls off the boil out of slow
corners.

The rest tames the pendulum. A **stiff front bar with a soft rear bar** plants the back, forward
brake bias keeps the featherweight tail in line, and **rear toe-in** holds it straight on the lift.
The **Differential** follows the site rule — more locked on loose, more open on grip — with the
coast side kept high to steady the car when you come off the throttle.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the lowest-grip surface, so soften the springs and bars and raise the ride height to clear
the ruts. Lock the diff up harder — on a loose surface a tight diff drives both rear wheels instead
of spinning the inside one away, and the rear weight gives you the traction to use it. Extra preload
and coast lock keep the light tail from stepping out when you lift.

With so little mass and grip the car can snap fast here. Get it settled and pointed before you feed
the power in, and keep steering inputs gentle.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel sits close to the hybrid setup and suits the Popper well — it is a tight, technical surface
where the car's darty rotation is an asset. The changes off the baseline are small: keep the front
bar stiff and the rear soft so the tail stays planted while the nose does the turning.

Small, smooth inputs win here. A rear-engine car this light will swap ends if you snap it from lock
to lock, which on a pad is easy to overdo.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

On asphalt the springs and bars stiffen and the car drops closer to the road. The **diff opens up**
— on high grip a locked diff breaks both rear wheels loose together, and this car needs no help
losing the rear. More front camber and brake bias let the nose bite so you turn on the front, not
the tail. The very short gearing still helps you claw back speed out of the corners.

The pendulum is the thing to watch: a sharp lift on turn-in pitches the rear out. Brake in a
straight line, be off the pedal before you commit, and let the grip carry you through.

</Fragment>
