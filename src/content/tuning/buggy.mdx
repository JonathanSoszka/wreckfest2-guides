<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The engine sits behind the rear axle, so the whole setup is about taming the tail rather than
freeing it. Weight over the back means the Buggy hooks up well on power, but lift off mid-corner and
that same mass swings the rear around like a pendulum. The two big levers pull against it: a **stiff
front bar with a soft rear bar** to plant the back, and **rear toe-in** to keep it pointed straight
when the weight shifts.

The **Differential** follows the site rule — more locked on loose, more open on grip — but the coast
side runs high everywhere, because it is coast lock that steadies the car on the lift that would
otherwise start the swing. Brake balance sits forward for the same reason: front brake keeps the
light rear in line under braking.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is low grip and full of ruts, so soften the springs and bars and raise the ride height to clear
the worst of it. Lock the diff up harder — on a loose surface a tight diff drives both rear wheels
instead of spinning the inside one away, and the rear weight already gives you traction to lean on.
More preload and coast lock hold the tail steady when you come off the throttle.

The pendulum is at its most vicious here because there is little grip to catch it. Lift off early and
gently, get the car settled before you turn, and feed the power back in smoothly.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel sits close to the hybrid setup — small moves off the baseline. The rear weight puts the power
down well and the car rotates willingly, so the job is keeping that rotation controlled rather than
chasing more of it. Keep the front bar stiff and the rear soft so the tail stays planted through the
middle of the corner.

Steer with small inputs and let the car settle between direction changes — a rear-engine car punishes
a fast flick from one lock to the other, which on a pad is easy to overdo.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

On asphalt there is real grip, so the springs and bars stiffen and the car drops closer to the road.
The **diff opens up** — on high grip a locked diff breaks both rear wheels loose together, and this
car needs no help breaking the rear loose. More front camber and brake bias help the nose bite so you
are not relying on the tail to turn.

The pendulum still lurks: a sharp lift on turn-in can pitch the rear out fast. Brake in a straight
line, get off the pedal before you commit to the corner, and the extra grip does the rest.

</Fragment>
