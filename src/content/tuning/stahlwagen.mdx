<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Stahlwagen is the stable one. A long wheelbase and wagon mass carried slightly rearward give it
calm, controllable balance and good rear traction, so nothing here is fighting a nervous car — which
means the fine controls matter more. **Brake balance** and the **Differential** do most of the work:
sitting near centre with a touch forward keeps it honest on entry, and moderate power lock lets the
rear weight put the torque down without snapping.

Everything else follows the surface: locked diff and soft/high on loose, opener diff and stiff/low
on grip. The anti-roll bars run close front-to-rear — this is a balanced wagon, not a car you tune
to rotate. The **Final Drive** gears for the torque-rich midrange. Brake Pressure's scale runs
50–150%, so 100% is mid-travel, not the maximum.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the lowest-grip surface, but the Stahlwagen's rearward weight helps it dig in and drive.
Soften the springs and bars, raise the ride height to clear the ruts, and **lock the diff up**: on
low grip a tight diff drives both rear wheels instead of spinning the inside one away, and the loose
surface still lets you steer with the throttle.

Lean on the wagon's stability — it stays composed where a nose-heavy car would flounder. Feed the
throttle smoothly and let its traction carry you out.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel is the middle ground and sits closest to the hybrid setup — the changes are small. The
Stahlwagen slides predictably and holds its line, so you can commit earlier than in a twitchier car;
keep corrections small, which matters on a pad where a big stick input overshoots.

Trust the balance and drive it clean. This is a car you place accurately rather than wrestle.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

On asphalt the Stahlwagen has real grip and its long wheelbase makes it planted and predictable. The
springs and bars stiffen, brake pressure climbs, and the car drops closer to the road — and the
**diff opens up**: on high grip a locked diff breaks both rear wheels loose together, so a looser
diff keeps the exit progressive.

Here the **brake bias** earns its keep: a touch forward stops the long tail from stepping out under
hard, late braking. Brake in a straight line and let the stability work for you.

</Fragment>
