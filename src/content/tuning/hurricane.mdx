<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

Two things drive the Hurricane's setup more than anything else.

The **Differential** runs opposite to instinct — **locked on loose, opener on grip.** On mud and
gravel, locking it drives both rear wheels for traction (the loose surface and the low-end torque
still rotate the car). On asphalt, the torque-forward rear snaps both wheels loose together if the
diff is tight, so open it up — further than the RoadSlayer, because the Hurricane bites harder.
Come down more still if the exit is catching you out on a pad.

The **Final Drive** matters more here than on most cars because the engine runs out of revs
early. Gearing it taller (toward top speed) keeps you in the fat part of the torque curve instead
of bouncing off the limiter down the straights.

Everything else follows the surface, trimmed for the car: a stiffer front anti-roll bar than the
rear to settle the mild entry understeer, and a soft rear bar so the rear hooks the torque up on exit.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

The Hurricane's lighter tail and low-down torque make it lively on mud — it rotates on the
throttle without much persuasion. Soften and raise everything to ride the ruts, and **lock the diff
up** for traction on the low grip: a tight diff drives both rear wheels instead of spinning the
inside one away, and the loose surface still lets you steer with the throttle.

Gear it long even here — the early redline still bites, and wheelspin on mud is lost drive, not
speed. Feed the throttle smoothly and let the rotation do the steering.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel is where the Hurricane is at its best, and it sits close to the hybrid setup. The power
oversteer becomes a tool — back off to set the nose, then feed the torque to rotate and drive out.

Keep the rear bar soft so the slide is progressive rather than a snap, which matters on a pad where
a big stick input overshoots.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt is where the torque-forward rear is most likely to bite. Stiffen the springs and front
bar, drop the car, and — the key move — **open the diff up** and keep the rear bar soft, so the
rear grips the tarmac and the power stays progressive instead of snapping both wheels loose together.

Gear it as long as the track allows to stay off the limiter, and keep some compliance for the
bumps and kerbs so a mid-corner hit does not snap the rear loose.

</Fragment>
