#### Why these numbers

Front-wheel drive inverts most of what you do to a rear-drive car. The front tyres already steer
**and** drive, so they are always the limiting factor; the whole job is to take work off the front
axle and hand it to the rear. Tune it like a muscle car and it understeers everywhere.

**Suspension and bars are the inversion made concrete: soft front, stiff rear.** Stiffen the rear
bar and springs to loosen the tail and point the nose; keep the front soft and compliant so the
driven wheels stay planted.

The **Differential** still locks on loose for traction, but opens further on grip than a rear-drive
car would — a tight driven diff on tarmac just adds understeer as it fights your steering. Keep the
coast side low: too much lock there makes the nose push on entry.

Brakes sit forward of a rear-drive car since the front does the stopping anyway — but keep a slice
of rear brake dialled in on purpose, because it helps the light tail rotate on entry.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud is the worst surface for a front-drive car — the driven front wheels dig in and understeer is
the default. You cannot power through it, so soften the front, raise the ride height, and **lock
the diff up** to pull the nose out of corners.

Brake early and light, and get all your rotation done on the entry lift; the setup only helps if
the technique matches it.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel is where a disciplined FWD driver is competitive, and it sits close to the hybrid setup.
Lift-off oversteer becomes the tool — back off to rotate the light rear, point the nose, then drive
out in a straight line.

Practise the rotate-then-drive rhythm until it is automatic; setup alone will not do it.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt's grip exposes FWD's exit understeer most clearly — the front runs out of grip trying to
steer and drive at once. Stiffen the rear further, lower the car, firm the front to use the grip,
and **open the diff** so it stops fighting your steering.

Add more front negative camber too, and keep a slice of rear brake so you do not lose the entry
rotation you rely on.

<div class="callout"><p>Have a car-specific guide? Those refine these numbers for a single car. Otherwise start here.</p></div>

Front-wheel-drive cars ask for the opposite of a muscle car: the enemy is **understeer**,
especially power-on understeer out of corners, and the light rear is a *tool* to fight it. The
**Hybrid** tab on the left is an all-round setup that does OK on any surface, in the game's real
sliders and units; each surface tab shows what to change from it. Gamepad players: Steering Ratio
one notch slower than shown.

## Reading the presets

The filled marker is the setting, the tick is the hybrid value, and the coloured number is the
change from it (green higher, blue lower). Each tab's own **Why these changes** panel — below its
settings — explains the reasoning for that surface.
