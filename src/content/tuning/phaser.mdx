<Fragment slot="hybrid">

#### Why these numbers

The Phaser is a nose-heavy corner-carver with a peaky, high-revving four. It makes its power up top,
so gear it short and keep the revs up — it will not lug out of a corner like a V8. The chassis is
nimble, but the light FWD rear can snap over kerbs and jumps, so the rear is firm to rotate the nose
yet trimmed back from a full-attack setting.

The **differential** governs the driven front wheels: locked on loose to put power down, opener on
grip so it stops fighting your steering. **Soft front, firm rear** points the nose, but on rough
tracks add a touch of rear ride height and a little rear toe-in to steady that light tail. Keep the
coast side low so the driven axle does not push on entry.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="mud">

#### Why these changes

Mud punishes the driven front — the wheels dig and the nose washes wide. Soften the front, raise the
ride height, and **lock the diff up** so both front wheels pull the car out.

Keep the revs up out of slow corners since the engine makes nothing down low, and get your rotation
done on the entry lift rather than trying to power the nose round.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="gravel">

#### Why these changes

Gravel suits the Phaser's agility and sits close to the hybrid setup. Lift-off rotation points the
nose neatly — back off to rotate, then feed the revs back in and drive out.

This is where the trimmed-back rear pays off: the light tail can snap over the rough stuff, so keep
the rear bar firm but not vicious, and keep pad corrections small.

</Fragment>
<Fragment slot="asphalt">

#### Why these changes

Asphalt exposes exit understeer as the loaded front runs out of grip. Stiffen the rear, drop the
front, firm the front to use the grip, and **open the diff** so it stops fighting your steering.

Add front negative camber to plant the outside edge, but keep the rear settings honest — Wreckfest
tarmac is kerbed and bumpy, and a light rear on a stiff bar will step out over a mid-corner bump.

</Fragment>
