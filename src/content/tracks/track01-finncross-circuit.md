FinnCross is the closest thing the game has to a proper rallycross circuit: a compact loop
that is tarmac for most of its length with a gravel section spliced in, so every lap you
cross the join between the two surfaces at least twice. It runs one main layout plus a
reverse, and because it is short you get a lot of laps to learn it — a good place to start.

## The surface joins are the track

On a single-surface circuit you can pick one braking reference and trust it everywhere. Here
you cannot. Grip on the tarmac is high and predictable; the gravel loop is loose, the car
moves around, and braking distances stretch. The corners that decide the lap are the ones
right at the transition — braking on tarmac into a gravel corner, or getting on the power on
gravel just before the track goes back to tarmac.

Treat the `asphalt_to_gravel` join like a braking marker. Do your hard braking while you are
still on tarmac where the grip is, then carry a lower, more patient entry speed through the
loose section. Trying to brake late on the gravel itself is the classic FinnCross mistake —
the car simply slides on past the apex.

## Running it in reverse

The reverse layout is slightly longer and tips a little more toward tarmac (73% versus 64%),
which changes where the loose section falls relative to the fast corners. Do not assume your
forward braking points mirror over — the gravel loop arrives at a different point in the lap,
so relearn the transitions specifically. Everything else you know about the venue carries.

## What to work on here

This is the ideal track to drill surface-change braking, which is the single most useful
skill on Wreckfest's mixed circuits. See [Braking on Loose Surfaces](/lessons/braking-on-loose-surfaces/).
Because you spend real time on both grip levels, a neutral, hybrid-leaning setup works better
than anything tuned for one surface — the [generic RWD guide](/tuning/generic-rwd/)'s hybrid
tab is a sensible starting point.
