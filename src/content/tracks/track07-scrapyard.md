Scrapyard is the demolition-flavoured venue: dusty dirt ground, walls of stacked junk cars,
and roads that cross over each other rather than looping cleanly. It runs a main route with a
reverse plus several more configurations, at least one of which is a criss-cross layout where
the track deliberately intersects itself.

## Where contact is the track, not a hazard

Most circuits punish contact; a scrapyard layout is built around it. The criss-cross sections
put cars on a collision course by design, and the tight junkyard corridors leave nowhere to
avoid a rival who wants to trade paint. Reading traffic — knowing who is about to cross your
path and deciding early whether to yield or commit — matters as much as your line.

The dirt surface is loose and dusty, so the same throttle patience that works on gravel
applies: rotate the car while slowing, then drive out straight. But you will rarely get a
clean lap here, so consistency under contact beats a perfect line you can never actually run.

## What to work on here

This is contact-racing practice above all. Learn to use a rival's car as a brake or a berm,
to take a hit without spinning, and to pick your moment through the crossings. See
[Racing With Contact](/lessons/racing-with-contact/). A robust, forgiving dirt setup that
stays composed when you get bumped is worth more here than an edgy fast one — start from the
gravel tab of the relevant [tuning guide](/tuning/).
