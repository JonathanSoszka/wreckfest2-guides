On tarmac you turn the car with the steering wheel. On loose surfaces that mostly stops working:
the front tyres skate across the top layer instead of biting into it, and adding lock just scrubs
more speed without changing your direction. Rally drivers get around this by making the rotation
happen deliberately, with the pedals and with weight, before the corner properly starts. The
brakes turn the car more than the wheel does.

## Lift, turn, wait

The simplest version is the one to learn first. Lift off the throttle and the car's weight moves
forward onto the front tyres, pressing them into the loose surface where they can finally get some
grip. That same transfer takes weight off the rear, so the back of the car goes light and starts to
come round. Lift, turn the wheel, then wait — the rotation arrives a beat later, and the common
mistake is to get impatient and add more steering in the meantime, which only washes the front out.

If the car isn't rotating enough, add a brush of brake instead of more lock. That is the same
transfer, harder. If it is rotating too much, come off the brake and the rear loads up again and
settles. Releasing the brake is how you stop the rotation, which makes brake release a steering
input in its own right.

## Left-foot braking

Left-foot braking is just doing the above without giving up drive: your left foot works the brake
while the right stays on the throttle, so you can load the front and rotate the car while still
pulling. Mid-corner understeer is what it fixes — a light dab shifts weight forward, the nose bites,
and the car tucks back to the line without you having to lift and lose all your momentum.

How much it helps depends on the car. It does the most in front-wheel drive, where it is essentially
a way to make the rear brakes do something about the car's built-in understeer while the front wheels
keep driving. It matters less in rear-wheel drive, which can already adjust its attitude on the
throttle — and rear-wheel drive is most of the Wreckfest field. There is no all-wheel drive in the
game, so the AWD-specific reasons rally drivers cite for this technique don't apply here.

On a gamepad this is nearly free. Throttle and brake are separate triggers, so overlapping them costs
you nothing mechanically — none of the footwork that makes it an advanced skill in a real car. Worth
a lap of testing that the game doesn't quietly cut throttle the moment you touch the brake.

## The flick

For a corner that is too tight to rotate any other way, there is the Scandinavian flick. Instead of
approaching from the outside, you come in from the inside, steer sharply away from the corner with a
touch of brake, and let the car's weight swing across onto the outside tyres. Then you snap the
steering back into the corner and release — the car pivots on the energy you just stored and arrives
already pointing at the exit.

Two honest caveats. It is a tool for tight, slow corners, not a way of taking every bend — on grippy
surfaces it shocks the front tyres and throws away more than it gains, and coaches disagree about how
much top-level rally still uses it at all. And it costs you speed on the way in. Use it where you were
never going to carry speed anyway.

## The handbrake, for corners that are barely corners

Hairpins and junction-tight turns don't give you enough entry speed to rotate with weight, because
there is no momentum to work with. That is what the handbrake is for. Slow down well before, come off
the footbrake, turn in, pull the lever, release as soon as the car has come round, and get back on the
throttle to turn the slide into drive. In a front-wheel-drive car you can leave the clutch alone —
locking the rears doesn't touch the driven wheels.

It scrubs a lot of speed, so it is a last resort rather than a habit. If you can rotate the car with a
lift and a brush of brake, that is always faster.

## How much angle is actually fast

Sliding looks fast and mostly isn't. The target is enough slip to get the car pointed and no more —
past that, the tyres are pushing sideways instead of forwards and you are just scrubbing off speed in
a spectacular way. Rotate the car, catch it, then straighten it and drive. If you are holding a big
angle all the way to the exit, you are giving away the corner.

## Practising it

Take one tight corner and work through the tools in order over a few laps: lift and wait, then lift
and brush the brake, then try the flick. You will feel which one that corner actually wants. Most
corners want the first two, and the ones that want the handbrake are obvious — they are the ones where
you have already almost stopped.
