A gravel road is a hard base with a layer of loose material sitting on top of it, and almost all of
the grip is in the base. The loose stuff on top behaves like marbles on a kitchen floor — you can
drive on it, but the tyres are riding across it rather than gripping through it. Most of what looks
like bad luck on dirt is really a failure to notice which of those two surfaces you are on.

## The grip is where the cars have been

As cars run a loose track they throw the top layer sideways, and the base starts showing through in
grooves and swept patches. Those swept areas are the grippiest part of the road, which is why the fast
line on dirt tends to follow the used line rather than a line you calculated. The material that got
thrown has to go somewhere, and it piles up off the line — thickest toward the edges, exactly where
you end up if you run wide.

This has a consequence worth internalising: grip is not the same across the width of the road, and it
is not even necessarily the same for both sides of your car at once. Nor is it the same on the way out
of a corner as it was on the way in. Treat every corner as something to read rather than something you
have already solved.

## The line moves during a race

Because the swept line is made by traffic, it develops as the race runs. The road on lap one is not
the road on lap six. Early on, the surface is loose and even; later, there is a defined groove that
rewards you for using it and punishes you for stepping out of it. Keep checking whether the line you
committed to on the opening lap is still the best one.

## Ruts

Soft, sandy sections get carved into ruts by repeated passes, and by mid-race they can genuinely
change what a corner asks of you. Be careful with the popular advice that ruts are a grippier rail to
lean on — professional rally coaching doesn't teach them that way. Ruts are better understood as a
constraint: they take away your freedom to choose a line, and at slow, tight corners they are a trap,
because the car has to climb out of one to go anywhere. Plenty of rally cars have ended their day by
sinking into a rut at a hairpin.

## Camber and the crown

The road's cross-slope changes your cornering speed more than most drivers expect. Where the road
slopes into the corner you are turning, it works like banking — the car is pressed into the surface and
you can carry noticeably more speed. Where it slopes away, everything has to be earlier and smoother,
and one useful trick is to apex an off-camber corner early so you reach the better-cambered part of
the road sooner.

The transition matters more than the slope itself. Crossing the crown in the middle of the road, the
car goes light for a moment and can hand you rotation you didn't ask for. At speed, treat a crown like
a small crest rather than like flat ground.

On loose surfaces the two effects stack against you at the outside of the corner: that is where the
camber is worst *and* where the thrown material is deepest. Running wide on dirt is punished twice.

## What cutting drags onto the line

Cutting the inside of a corner is normal and often fast, but it is also how the road gets ruined.
Every cut pulls stones and dirt out of the inside and deposits them on the racing line for the next
lap — including your own. In real rallying this is the main source of punctures, and the inside of a
bend is where organisers put logs and rocks precisely because they know drivers will go there. In
Wreckfest the debris matters less than the contact does, but the pattern still holds: the tidy inside
line you found on lap two may be a mess by lap five, because you and everyone else keep dragging it
out there.

## Practising it

Spend a race deliberately watching the road instead of the car. Notice where the groove is forming,
which corners have gone rutted, and which way the road slopes at the point you turn in. Then try one
lap running slightly off your usual line toward the swept part and see whether the car takes it
better. On most loose tracks it will.
