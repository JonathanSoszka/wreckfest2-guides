No two corners are quite the same, but they fall into a handful of shapes, and each shape asks for a
slightly different line. Learn to read which one you are looking at and the approach mostly picks
itself. One rule sits above all of them: win the exit. The corner that feeds a straight sets your speed
for the whole next section, so a tidy exit beats a fast apex almost every time — slow in, fast out.

## Hairpins

A hairpin is a very tight, slow, almost-180° turn, and it is the most extreme case of slow-in/fast-out.
Brake early and hard in a straight line, take a patient entry, clip a late apex, get the car rotated,
and then feed the power out. Rushing the entry just runs you wide and wastes the exit — see
[the racing line](/lessons/the-racing-line/) and [throttle control](/lessons/throttle-control-and-corner-exit/).

## Sweepers

A sweeper is a long, fast, roughly constant curve. There is little braking to do — the skill is
carrying momentum on one smooth arc with a steady throttle and small hands. Pick your line and commit
to it; sawing at the wheel mid-sweeper scrubs speed and unsettles the car.

## Chicanes and esses

A chicane is a quick linked left-right (or right-left). Brake in a straight line before it, then
**sacrifice the first apex** to straighten the whole run and set up a clean exit from the last part —
that last element is the one feeding the straight. Manage the side-to-side weight transfer between the
direction changes and don't over-rotate into the second half.

## The radius trap: decreasing vs increasing

Where a corner is sharpest decides where you apex. An **increasing-radius** corner is sharpest early
and then opens up, so you can apex earlier and get on the power sooner. A **decreasing-radius** corner
tightens toward the exit, and it is the classic trap: apex early and the tightening wall runs you
straight off the outside. Treat a corner you are unsure about as decreasing — brake a little deeper, be
patient, take a late apex, and wait for it to open before you commit the throttle.

## Off-camber, crests, and blind corners

These are the ones that catch people out. An **off-camber** corner slopes away from the turn, so grip
drops and the car pushes wide — slow more, trail a little more brake, and be gentle. Over a **crest**
the car goes light (the front unloads and pushes, then the rear unloads and steps out), so get your
braking and most of your steering done before or after the rise, not while the car is floating; a later
apex helps. On a **blind** corner you cannot see the exit, so lean on reference points and don't commit
to a line you cannot yet see — doubly true on Wreckfest's rougher tracks.

## On loose surfaces

Everything above still applies on gravel and dirt, but grip is lower and always shifting, so lean
harder on the late-apex "square-off" and do more of your turning with the car rotating. Rotate it on
entry — a lift or a trailed brake to bring the tail around — then steer it out on the throttle, the way
[catching a slide](/lessons/catching-a-slide/) and [surface transitions](/lessons/driving-surface-transitions/)
describe. For tight corners and hairpins on the loose, the rally tool is the **Scandinavian flick**
(the pendulum turn): flick the car the "wrong" way first so the weight transfer swings the tail, then
turn in and drive out. It is the loose-surface way through a hairpin that would otherwise need a crawl.
On a gamepad, keep every version of this small and early — the flick works, but a big stab overshoots.
