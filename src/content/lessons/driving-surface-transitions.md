Mixed tracks hand you two different cars in one lap. Tarmac gives high, predictable grip where
the car stays planted and stops short. The moment you cross onto gravel or dirt the grip drops,
the car starts moving around under you, and braking distances stretch. The mistake is driving the
whole corner as if the grip never changed — the fast approach is to know exactly where the surface
flips and set the car up for the side you are landing on.

## Brake on the grip, coast onto the loose

Do your hard braking while you are still on the grippy side, where the tyres can actually take it.
Get the speed off early, then carry a lower, more patient entry speed onto the loose side and let
the car settle. Braking late so that you are still hauling the car down as you cross onto gravel
just locks the wheels and slides you past the apex. Shed the speed where the grip is, not where it
has already gone.

## Treat the join like a braking marker

The edge of the tarmac is a marker as real as any board or cone. Anticipate it, expect the car to
move as the grip falls away, and lift a fraction earlier than feels necessary. If you wait to
react until you feel the back end go light, you are already late — the correction comes before the
join, not after it.

## Getting back on the power

Coming the other way — feeding throttle on the loose just before it turns to tarmac — asks for the
same patience. Wait for the grip before you commit. Squeeze in gently while you are still on gravel
and let the front hook up as the surface firms, rather than burying the throttle on the loose and
spinning the rears right as the tarmac arrives.

## Set up for both, not one

Because you spend real time on both grip levels every lap, a setup tuned hard for a single surface
fights you on the other. A neutral, hybrid-leaning balance carries better across the whole track —
the [hybrid tab of the generic RWD guide](/tuning/generic-rwd/) is a sensible starting point.

## On a gamepad

The surface change is exactly where a coarse input bites hardest — the car is at its most
unsettled right as your stick or trigger is least precise. Keep corrections small and early, roll
into the join instead of jerking at it, and lean on the site's Steering Ratio note (one notch
slower than shown) to take the edge off. This ties straight into
[braking on loose surfaces](/lessons/braking-on-loose-surfaces/), and the tracks built around these
transitions are [FinnCross](/tracks/track01-finncross-circuit/) and
[European Rallycross](/tracks/track05-european-rallycross/).
