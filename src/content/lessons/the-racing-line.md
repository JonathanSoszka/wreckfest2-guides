On a grippy circuit the fast line is one smooth arc — roughly outside, clip the apex, run
out to the outside again — because a single flowing curve carries the most speed when the
tyres have grip to spare. That instinct is worth unlearning on most of Wreckfest's tracks.
They run loose or mixed, and on low grip the arc that looks fastest is the one that gets you
slowed and pointed before you ask for power.

## Square the corner off

The rally line squares the corner off. Instead of one long curve, you keep the entry
straighter, brake deeper toward the corner, then turn to a later and tighter apex so the car
is already pointed down the exit before you get on the throttle. It looks slower through the
middle and it is faster out of it. The straightening is the whole point: it lets the tyres do
one job at a time — brake, then turn, then accelerate — instead of asking for two at once on a
surface that has no grip to spare. On loose and mixed tracks, favour squaring the corner off
over chasing a perfect geometric arc.

## Why it wins on low grip

A tyre only has so much grip to give, and on a loose surface that pool is small. Overlap
braking and cornering the way a circuit line does and something lets go — the front washes
wide or the rear steps out. The late apex separates the phases so each one gets the tyre to
itself. Get the slowing done in a straight line, turn while you are off the brakes, then
unwind the lock as you feed the throttle in one smooth build. That sequence is what the
squared-off line is built to give you.

## The line is not fixed

The ideal line moves as a race wears on. Ruts and swept grooves cut down to grippier base
material, so the fastest path drifts off the polished, scrubbed line that everyone has been
sliding across. A rutted inside or a line one car-width off the obvious groove can have more
bite late in a race than it did on lap one. Spend a lap experimenting rather than assuming the
worn-in line is the quick one.

## Learn it, then adapt it

In a pack the ideal line is rarely yours to take — someone is on it, or about to put you into
the wall for it. Learn the clean squared-off line first so you know what you are giving up,
then adjust it under contact: a tighter, more defensive entry, or a wider one to carry speed
around trouble. On a gamepad the corrections that keep you on your improvised line want to be
small and early, so pick your compromise before you turn in, not halfway through.

## Practising it

Take a mixed circuit like [FinnCross](/tracks/track01-finncross-circuit/) and run one corner
two ways. First drive the geometric arc, one smooth curve. Then deliberately keep the entry
straight, brake later toward the corner, and turn to an apex a few metres past where you took
it before. Feel how much earlier you can get to full throttle when the car is already pointed.
The squared line pairs with [braking on loose surfaces](/lessons/braking-on-loose-surfaces/)
and with reading [driving surface transitions](/lessons/driving-surface-transitions/) as the
grip changes under you.
