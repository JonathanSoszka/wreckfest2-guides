Rallycross runs on circuits under a mile long, and the reason the standing start gets so much attention
there is simple: when a lap is that short, passing chances are few, so the positions you take in the
first ten seconds tend to be the positions you keep. Wreckfest's tracks are the same kind of short, and
a full grid arriving at the same corner together makes the opening seconds worth more than any single
corner later on.

## The launch

A good launch is a traction problem, not a reaction-time problem. Rallycross cars will do 0–60 in under
two seconds, and the driver spends that time acting as their own traction control — feeding power in at
the rate the surface will take, and no faster. Bury the throttle on loose ground and the wheels light
up, the car squats and goes nowhere while everyone around you leaves.

So the loose-surface start is the same shape as the loose-surface corner exit: one smooth, progressive
build rather than an instant commitment. If you can feel the wheels spinning, you have already given
away the launch — ease off slightly and let them hook up rather than waiting for the car to sort itself
out. Grip also changes as a race meeting goes on, so a launch that worked earlier may need less throttle
later, or more.

## Turn one is its own skill

Launching well and surviving the first corner are different problems, and the second one is where races
are actually lost. Everyone arrives at once, nobody has track position yet, and the braking zone is
full of cars who all believe they have the line. Even at professional level this goes wrong routinely —
a World RX final once lost three of its front-runners at turn one despite the two leaders having agreed
beforehand to leave each other room.

The useful instinct is to decide early whether you are winning this corner or surviving it. If you got
a good launch and you are clear, take the normal line and get out of the mess. If you are in the middle
of the pack, brake earlier than you would on a hot lap and aim for the gap that will still exist in a
second's time — not the one that is open right now. Being second through turn one and still pointing
forwards beats being first into it and getting spun.

Watch the inside. It is the obvious place for someone to lunge, it is where the braking is worst on
loose ground, and it is where a car that has overcooked its entry will end up — usually across your nose.

## What being ahead is actually worth

Clear air is the real prize, and on a loose surface it is worth more than it looks. Running in traffic
on dirt means driving through everyone else's thrown material with a dirty screen and no clean view of
the road — the loose-surface version of dirty air. Getting out of that early is a large part of why the
start matters so much.

## Practising it

Run the same track a few times and pay attention only to the first ten seconds. Try a launch with less
throttle than feels right and see whether you actually lose anything. Then pick your turn-one approach
before the lights rather than improvising it — you will find the earlier, more conservative entry gains
places more often than the aggressive one does, simply because so many cars ahead of you won't make it
through cleanly.

Contact in that first corner is a near-certainty rather than a risk; how to take it and give it is
covered in [Racing With Contact](/lessons/racing-with-contact/).
