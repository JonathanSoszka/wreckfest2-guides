Circuit racing teaches you that off the racing line is where the grip runs out. On loose and mixed
surfaces that intuition is often backwards, and unlearning it is most of what makes overtaking work
here. The rule that replaces it is blunter: go where the grip is, and work out where that is by looking
at the road rather than assuming.

## Off-line can be the better line

On tarmac, the marbles off-line are worn rubber and they are always bad. On dirt, the "marbles" are the
loose top layer, and the swept groove where the cars run is where the base shows through — but that
groove is only as wide as the traffic that made it, and it does not always sit where the geometric line
would. Slightly off the obvious line there is often clean, compacted surface that nobody is using,
because everyone is following the car in front out of habit.

That is your opening. A pass on a loose track frequently comes from taking a line the driver ahead has
written off, not from out-braking them on the one they are defending.

## The surface change is the passing place

On a track that mixes tarmac and dirt, the transitions are where the biggest gains and the worst
mistakes happen. Grip changes in a step, and so does how the car answers your inputs — a car that is
sideways and happy on gravel is a car that suddenly has traction and snaps straight when it hits
tarmac. Drivers who tidy up before the change and cross it neatly gain time on drivers who arrive at it
still rotating.

So look for the transition on your lap and treat it as a corner in its own right. If the driver ahead
is untidy there — and most are — that is a repeatable place to be closer at the exit than you were at
the entry, which is what sets up the pass into the following corner.

One specific trick worth knowing: where a strip of firmer surface runs down the inside of a loose
corner, a slower, tighter entry that gets you onto it can beat a faster entry that leaves you on the
loose outside, because you can get on the power much earlier. You give up entry speed and take back
more on exit.

## The undercut

The other reliable pass on a short mixed track is not a pass at all in the corner where it starts.
Sacrifice your entry into one corner, get the car straight and rotated early, and use the drive out of
it to arrive alongside at the next one. On loose surfaces this works better than a late lunge, because
exits are worth so much more than apexes when traction is the limiting factor and the following straight
is short.

## Being behind costs more than a position

Following closely on dirt has a real cost beyond track position: you are driving through everything the
car ahead is throwing at you, with a filthy screen and a poor read of the surface. Professional
rallycross drivers describe exactly this as the reason to get clear rather than settle in behind. If you
cannot pass within a lap or two, it is often better to drop back slightly for a clean view of the road
and set up one good attempt than to sit in the spray losing information about where the grip is.

## Defending

Defending on a loose surface is mostly about denying the good surface rather than blocking the road.
Take the swept line, be early and tidy through the transition so there is no run on you, and be aware
that the inside you are covering may not be the grippy part anyway. What you cannot do is defend by
being untidy — on the loose, a car that is sliding around to hold position is a car that is slow on
exit, and it will simply be passed on the next straight instead.

The contact side of all this — how much is fair, and how to take a hit without losing the car — is
covered in [Racing With Contact](/lessons/racing-with-contact/).

## Practising it

Pick a mixed track and spend a race ignoring lap time. Watch where the groove actually runs, find the
surface transitions, and try one lap taking a deliberately unusual line into a corner you normally
follow traffic through. You are looking for the places where the fast line and the obvious line are not
the same thing — those are where your passes will come from.
