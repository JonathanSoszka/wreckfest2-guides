A jump is the one part of a lap where your inputs stop mattering. In the air you cannot slow down, you
cannot change direction, and you cannot fix your attitude — the car will do whatever the takeoff told
it to do. Everything that decides a good landing happens in the metre or two before the lip.

## What you actually control

You control two things: how fast you arrive, and how the car is pitched when it leaves. Speed sets
where you land. Pitch sets whether you land on all four wheels or on your nose. It is worth being
clear that these are separate — lifting off the throttle on the lip does not meaningfully shorten the
jump, it just tips the car forward. The arc is much the same either way; the attitude is not.

## Don't lift on the lip

The standard rally instruction is to stay on the throttle as you leave, which keeps weight toward the
rear and stops a nose-heavy car diving. Lift right at the lip and you throw weight forward at the exact
moment the suspension unloads, and the car pitches down and stays there. Landing front-first is how you
break things, lose steering, and arrive at the next corner already in trouble.

Treat this as a reliable rule of thumb rather than a law — it exists to counteract nose-dive, so it
matters most in cars that are heavy at the front. A quick dab and release of the brakes just before the
lip is the more advanced version: the front rebounds upward as you release, which pitches the nose up a
little on the way off.

## Landing

Come down with a little throttle rather than a lot, and rather than none. A small amount helps the
wheels match the speed of the ground, so the tyres are already turning when they arrive and the car
settles instead of snatching. Full throttle on touchdown just spins the wheels up and lights up the
rear; nothing at all lands you on locked wheels that have to be dragged back up to speed.

Try to land straight. A car that touches down with some angle on will use that angle the instant it has
grip, and on a loose surface it will keep going that way.

## Crests you cannot see over

A blind crest is the same problem with worse information. Braking is something you do before it, not on
it — the car goes light over the top, and a car that is light and being braked will lock up and stop
steering. Get your slowing done on the approach, be straight over the top, and pick the speed you can
live with for whatever is on the other side.

Rally crews handle this by writing the far side of every crest into their notes. You have the same
advantage after a couple of laps: once you know what follows, the crest stops being a gamble and becomes
just another commitment. Until then, be conservative — the cost of arriving too fast at something you
cannot see is much higher than the tenth you save.

## Practising it

Find a jump you keep landing badly. Do a few laps deliberately holding the throttle over the lip and
watch what the nose does, then a few deliberately lifting, and you will see the pitch difference
immediately. Then work on the landing: get the car straight before the lip and it will almost always
land straight too.
