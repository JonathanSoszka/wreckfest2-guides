Oversteer is the rear of the car stepping past its grip and trying to overtake the front.
It happens constantly on Wreckfest's loose surfaces, and a slide you catch cleanly is fast —
it points the nose and scrubs speed for you. A slide you fumble spins you or throws you into
the wall. The difference is a handful of habits, and all of them are easier the earlier you
react.

## Countersteer into the slide

When the rear breaks away, steer into it — turn the wheel the same way the back is sliding.
Match the amount to how far the car has rotated and apply it a touch faster than the car is
turning: a 30-degree slide wants about **30 degrees** of correction, put in quicker than the
yaw so you get ahead of it. Too little and the slide keeps growing; the goal is to meet the
rotation, not chase it.

## Look where you want to go

Fix your eyes down the road, at the exit you are aiming for — not at the wall you are trying
to miss. Your hands follow your eyes without you thinking about it, so a locked-in gaze on the
right line does half the correcting for you. Staring at what you want to avoid is the surest
way to steer straight into it.

## Mind the throttle

In a rear-drive car, do not snap fully off the power. A hard lift throws weight forward, the
rear goes light, and the car whips back the other way — the pendulum that turns one slide into
a worse one. Hold the throttle or ease it gently to keep the rear settled. In a front-drive
car it is the opposite: a gentle squeeze of throttle actually pulls you straight, so feed a
little power rather than lifting.

## Don't over-correct

The classic spin is too much opposite lock. Once you pile in more correction than the slide
needs, the car snaps back, loads the other side, and throws a harder secondary slide you have
even less time to catch. Put in enough to meet the rotation, then **unwind smoothly** as the
car straightens — the correction should come back out as calmly as it went in.

## On a gamepad

The stick is coarse, so make inputs small and quick rather than big and slow, and catch the
slide early while the correction is still tiny — a slide you meet at 10 degrees needs a flick;
one you meet at 40 needs a fistful and precise timing you probably don't have on a pad. Running
Steering Ratio one notch slower than shown gives you finer control near centre, which is exactly
where these corrections live.

This matters most on cars that are keen to swap ends: rear-engine pendulums like the
[Buggy](/cars/buggy/) and light, snap-happy cars like the [Jackal](/cars/jackal/) will teach
you fast. If a car is fighting you more than you can hold, the [tuning guides](/tuning/) cover
setups that calm the rear down.
